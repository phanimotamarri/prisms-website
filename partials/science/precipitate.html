Precipitate Evolution

Nanoscale precipitates are one of the most important and potent sources of strengthening in many metal alloys. Quantitative prediction of precipitate evolution is a long-standing scientific and technological problem in metal alloys. Exceptionally high strength in Mg alloys can be achieved by a very particular, complex and poorly understood precipitation sequence exhibited by a variety of Mg-rare earth (RE) alloys.

Figure Caption: Transmission electron microscope image of β’ precipitates in a Mg-Nd alloy (E. Sitzmann & E. Marquis, PRISMS Center).

Fundamental questions remain that need to be answered in order to enable the discovery and design of new Mg alloys with optimal strength. For example, how can precipitation mechanisms be described theoretically such that the evolution during aging heat treatments can be predicted; and what, in particular, about the rare-earth elements causes this favorable precipitation morphology and orientation and are there other non-rare-earth elements that will exhibit the same behavior.

Figure Caption. β’ precipitate distribution predicted for a Mg-Nd alloy using the PRISMS Phase Field code (Preliminary computation by L. Aageson and S. Rudruraju PRISMS Center)

Developing predictive capabilities that address these and other questions is the goal of the PRISMS Precipitate Evolution Use Group. Our initial demonstration of this integrated framework is on β’ and β1 precipitate evolution in a Mg-Nd alloy. This is being conducted in conjunction with the Tensile Behavior use group and used to develop an integrated capability for predicting the influence of precipitates on strengthening and ductility of Mg-RE alloys.

Integration Approach

Outputs from statistical mechanics calculations determined using the PRISMS Statistical Mechanics Code (CASM) are input to the PRISMS phase field code (PRISMS-PF). Specifically, first principles calculations using VASP are used to fit a cluster expansion for the hcp alloy system that will be used in Monte Carlo calculations of the free energy. A series of thermodynamic and kinetic models with varying levels of approximations are being parameterized in turn. Nuclei will be introduced to PRISMS-PF using explicit nucleation events. Experimental results from advanced transmission electron microscopy and atom probe tomography methods are used to identify the crystal structure, morphology and composition of precipitates to inform and validate models.
