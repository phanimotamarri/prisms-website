Integrated Leading Edge Science

To advance and demonstrate our predictive understanding in important scientific areas of research in metals, the PRISMS Center is organized around USE CASES involving our predictive scientific understanding of the metallic element magnesium and it's alloys. The PRISMS capability is meant to be generally applicable to any metallic system, however we are focused on magnesium as a demonstration of these capabilities.

Magnesium (Mg) present an important test case for accelerated material development. Mg alloys have significant potential for reducing the weight of automobiles and other transportation systems â€“ and thus for improving fuel economy and reducing emissions. However the behavior of Mg alloys presents a wide array of unsolved scientific challenges, including complex precipitation reactions, dislocation interactions in this hexagonal close packed (HCP) system, twin formation and growth and the influence of microstructure at all levels on tensile and fatigue behavior. Relative to other structural metals, the development of commercial Mg alloys and our understanding of Mg alloy physical metallurgy are relatively immature. While modern alloys of aluminum and nickel can have five to ten alloying elements, Mg alloys have remained simple and are typically based on only two or three alloying additions.

PRISMS Center integrated scientific research is organized around four USE CASES: precipitate evolution recrystallization and grain growth tensile behavior fatigue behavior

These Use Cases serve as demonstrations and test beds for development of the PRISMS framework. They also serve as the focal point for our highly integrated and collaborative scientific workflows and provide a basis and approach for establishing group consensus on goals, priorities and scientific approaches. The four use cases are also integrated into a comprehensive whole, with understanding and results from one use case being used as inputs to others so that the influence of alloying and processing on microstructures can be incorporated into prediction of mechanical properties.
